# Section 1: Introduction to ITIL (Version 5)

## 1.1 What is ITIL (Version 5)?

ITIL (Version 5) is the latest evolution of the globally recognised IT service management framework. Officially launched by PeopleCert on 12 February 2026, it extends ITIL 4 by natively integrating AI governance, a product and service lifecycle model, and experience-driven metrics - while preserving the 34 core management practices that practitioners worldwide rely on.

The framework has been updated to reflect how organisations actually operate in complex, AI-enabled digital ecosystems. Rather than replacing ITIL 4 outright, ITIL (Version 5) responds to three major forces that have reshaped IT service delivery since 2019:

- The acceleration of digital product delivery - organisations must now deliver services at unprecedented speed
- The explosion of AI technologies - automation, generative AI, and intelligent agents are now mainstream in service operations
- The growing importance of experience - technical performance alone is no longer sufficient; user and customer experience are measurable business outcomes

> **Content Composition: What Changed from ITIL 4?**
>
> - **40%** Retained directly from ITIL 4 - unchanged concepts and practices
> - **24%** Updated - existing ITIL 4 concepts revised or recontextualised
> - **36%** New - content that did not exist in ITIL 4 in any form
>
> *Source: PeopleCert, ITIL (Version 5) Foundation official publication, February 2026*

## 1.2 Why Version 5 Now?

ITIL 4 (released 2019) was a significant evolution from v3, introducing the Service Value System, guiding principles, and a flexible, non-prescriptive approach to value creation. However, seven years of digital transformation have exposed gaps - particularly around:

- The absence of a clear front-end to the value chain (what happens before design begins?)
- AI adoption without a governance framework
- The need to formally measure and demonstrate value, not just deliver services
- The difficulty smaller organisations had in knowing where to start with the flexible SVS model

ITIL (Version 5) addresses all of these directly.

## 1.3 What Stays the Same

A significant portion of what ITIL 4 practitioners know directly transfers. The following are retained in full:

- All 34 ITIL 4 management practices - five have been recategorised but none eliminated
- The 7 Guiding Principles - unchanged in content and priority
- The Service Value System (SVS) — updated but retained as the overarching model
- The Four Dimensions of Service Management — updated for AI and digital contexts

> **The 7 Guiding Principles (Unchanged)**
>
> 1. Focus on Value
> 2. Start Where You Are
> 3. Progress Iteratively with Feedback
> 4. Collaborate and Promote Visibility
> 5. Think and Work Holistically
> 6. Keep It Simple and Practical
> 7. Optimize and Automate

## 1.4 Certification Pathway

ITIL (Version 5) follows a structured certification pathway:

| Certification Level | Release Date / Notes |
|---|---|
| Foundation (Version 5) | 12 February 2026 — Entry point for all learners |
| Foundation Bridge (ITIL 4 holders) | 26 February 2026 — Focuses on the 36% new content |
| ITIL Product / Service / Experience | 12 March 2026 — Advanced practice tracks |
| ITIL Strategy / Transformation | 9 April 2026 — Leadership and change tracks |
| Managing Professional (MP) Transition | 14 May 2026 — For ITIL 4 MP holders |

> **For ITIL 4 Foundation Holders**
>
> Your ITIL 4 Foundation certificate is fully recognised as a prerequisite for all advanced ITIL (Version 5) qualifications. You do NOT need to retake the Foundation exam. The Foundation Bridge exam (released 26 Feb 2026) is your upgrade path — it focuses on the 36% genuinely new content. ITIL 4 and ITIL (Version 5) will coexist for a minimum of 12 months. No retirement date for ITIL 4 has been announced.

# Section 2: The 8-Stage Product and Service Lifecycle

The most significant structural change in ITIL (Version 5) is the replacement of the Service Value Chain with the 8-Stage Product and Service Lifecycle. This is the section that requires the most attention for practitioners trained on ITIL 4.

The lifecycle does not eliminate the Service Value Chain's flexibility - it provides a sequenced model that gives organisations, especially those new to ITSM, a clear path to follow. Experienced teams will recognise activities they already perform; the lifecycle names them, orders them, and assigns governance accountability to each.

> **The Eight Stages at a Glance**
>
> 1. **Discover** — Identify needs and opportunities
> 2. **Design** — Translate needs into specifications
> 3. **Acquire** — Obtain components (people, tech, suppliers)
> 4. **Build** — Develop or configure the service
> 5. **Transition** — Move to live operation
> 6. **Operate** — Run the service in production
> 7. **Deliver** — Realise and demonstrate value
> 8. **Support** — Sustain and continually improve

---

## Stage 1: Discover

Identify business needs, opportunities, and demand signals before any design work begins. This stage formalises the front end of the value stream.

**Key Activities:**
- Conduct stakeholder interviews and business needs analysis
- Map demand signals from customers and internal teams
- Assess market trends, technology opportunities and strategic fit
- Produce a Demand & Opportunity Register as the stage output
- Gate check: Is there a validated business case to proceed?

### Learning Objectives - Stage 1: Discover

- Explain the purpose and inputs of the Discover stage
- Identify who the key stakeholders are and their roles
- Describe how demand is captured and prioritised
- Distinguish Discover from adjacent stages (Design, Strategy)

### Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Demand Signal** | An indication of a business need or opportunity triggering the lifecycle |
| **Opportunity Register** | Documented log of identified potential services or improvements |
| **Stakeholder** | Any individual or group with an interest in the service outcome |
| **Value Co-creation** | Collaborative approach to defining what 'value' means for all parties |

> **Practitioner Note:** The Discover stage formalises what ITIL 4 left implicit. Many organisations already do this intuitively — ITIL (Version 5) gives it structure and accountability.

---

## Stage 2: Design

Translate identified needs into service specifications and architectures. Aligns with ITIL v3's Service Design phase, now integrated into a continuous lifecycle.

**Key Activities:**
- Define service requirements, SLAs, and quality criteria
- Design architecture across the Four Dimensions (Technology, People, Partners, Processes)
- Address security, risk, compliance, and accessibility from the outset
- Produce a Service Design Package (SDP) as stage output
- In AI-native contexts: design AI agent interactions and data flows

### Learning Objectives — Stage 2: Design

- Describe the outputs of the Design stage and their purpose
- Apply the Four Dimensions Model to a design scenario
- Explain how AI integration considerations are embedded in Design
- Identify common design risks and how they are mitigated

### Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Service Design Package (SDP)** | Comprehensive documentation defining all aspects of a service and its requirements |
| **Four Dimensions Model** | People & Orgs, Information & Tech, Partners & Suppliers, Value Streams & Processes |
| **Utility** | Functionality offered — 'fit for purpose' |
| **Warranty** | Assurance the service meets agreed requirements — 'fit for use' |

> **Practitioner Note:** Design is not a one-time event. ITIL (Version 5) treats it as a recurring stage in a continuous lifecycle, triggered whenever a service needs significant change.

---

## Stage 3: Acquire

Obtain the components needed to build the service - technology, people, and suppliers. A new explicit stage not previously formalised in ITIL 4.

**Key Activities:**
- Evaluate build vs buy vs partner decisions
- Run procurement and vendor selection processes
- Establish contracts, SLAs, and underpinning agreements (UAs)
- On-board new team members or third-party resources
- ITIL 4 addressed this within the Four Dimensions - now it is a lifecycle stage in its own right

### Learning Objectives — Stage 3: Acquire

- Explain why Acquire is a standalone stage in ITIL (Version 5)
- Describe the role of supplier management in this stage
- Distinguish between UAs, OLAs, and external contracts
- Apply criteria for build/buy/partner decisions

### Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Underpinning Agreement (UA)** | Agreement with a third party supporting an SLA commitment |
| **Operational Level Agreement (OLA)** | Internal agreement between teams supporting service delivery |
| **Supplier Management** | Practice ensuring third-party contributions meet agreed levels |
| **Sourcing Strategy** | Documented approach to how service components will be obtained |

> **Practitioner Note:** The explicit Acquire stage closes a gap in ITIL 4 where procurement decisions were often managed outside the framework without clear governance.

---

## Stage 4: Build

Develop or configure the service components. In AI-native environments, this increasingly includes configuring AI agents and automated workflows.

**Key Activities:**
- Develop, configure, or integrate service components
- Perform unit testing, integration testing, and user acceptance testing
- Configure AI agents, automation rules, and digital workflows
- Maintain a build log and traceability to Design stage outputs
- Handover criteria: all acceptance tests passed, documentation complete

### Learning Objectives — Stage 4: Build

- Describe the key activities within the Build stage
- Explain how AI configuration differs from traditional service build
- Identify the quality gates between Build and Transition
- Apply software development and DevOps concepts to service build

### Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Acceptance Testing** | Formal validation that a service meets agreed requirements before transition |
| **Configuration Item (CI)** | Any component that needs to be managed to deliver a service |
| **DevOps** | Cultural and technical approach integrating development and operations for faster delivery |
| **AI Agent Configuration** | Defining the rules, data access, and escalation paths for automated AI workers |

> **Practitioner Note:** Build is increasingly where AI capabilities are first deployed. Governance of AI outputs starts here - not after go-live.

---

## Stage 5: Transition

Move the service from development to live operation. Maps to ITIL v3's Service Transition and ITIL 4's Change Enablement and Release Management practices.

**Key Activities:**
- Plan and communicate the transition to all affected stakeholders
- Execute change enablement controls and approval gates
- Release to production using agreed release management processes
- Update the CMDB and all relevant service documentation
- Conduct post-implementation review within agreed timeframe

### Learning Objectives — Stage 5: Transition

- Describe the purpose and scope of the Transition stage
- Apply Change Enablement practice to a transition scenario
- Distinguish between standard, normal, and emergency changes
- Explain the role of Release Management in this stage

### Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Change Enablement** | Practice ensuring changes are assessed, authorised, and implemented with minimal risk |
| **Release Management** | Practice making new and changed services available for use |
| **CMDB** | Configuration Management Database — the authoritative record of CIs and relationships |
| **Post-Implementation Review (PIR)** | Structured assessment of the transition outcome against planned objectives |

> **Practitioner Note:** Transition remains the highest-risk stage. The most common cause of major incidents is still change — Change Enablement is not optional overhead.

---

## Stage 6: Operate

Run the service in live production. Incident Management, Request Fulfilment, and Event Management all live here - practices retained wholesale from ITIL 4.

**Key Activities:**
- Monitor services via Event Management and observability tooling
- Manage incidents using agreed classification and escalation procedures
- Fulfil service requests within agreed timescales
- Apply Problem Management to eliminate recurring failures
- AI in Operate: automated triage, virtual agents, predictive alerting

### Learning Objectives — Stage 6: Operate

- Describe the core practices active in the Operate stage
- Distinguish between an incident, a problem, and a known error
- Explain how AI augments - but does not replace — Operate practices
- Apply escalation and prioritisation criteria to incident scenarios

### Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Incident** | Unplanned interruption or quality reduction of a service |
| **Problem** | Root cause of one or more incidents |
| **Known Error** | A problem with a documented root cause and workaround |
| **Event Management** | Practice monitoring services and filtering events requiring action |

> **Practitioner Note:** Operate contains the practices most people encounter daily. ITIL (Version 5) does not change these fundamentally - it adds AI-augmentation guidance and links them explicitly to the Deliver stage.

---

## Stage 7: Deliver

Realise and demonstrate the value the service was intended to create. New in ITIL (Version 5) - what ITIL 4 described as an outcome is now a formal lifecycle phase.

**Key Activities:**
- Measure and report on value realisation against original business case
- Conduct service reviews with customers and sponsors
- Manage experience metrics: user satisfaction, NPS, adoption rates
- Link operational data (Operate stage) to value evidence
- Gate check: Is the service delivering its intended outcomes?

### Learning Objectives — Stage 7: Deliver

- Explain why Deliver is a distinct stage from Operate
- Describe the key metrics used in the Deliver stage
- Apply experience-driven measurement to a service scenario
- Distinguish value realisation reporting from operational reporting

### Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Value Realisation** | The extent to which intended business outcomes have been achieved |
| **Experience Metrics** | Measures of how users and customers perceive and feel about a service |
| **Service Review** | Formal meeting between provider and customer to assess service performance |
| **Net Promoter Score (NPS)** | Measure of user likelihood to recommend the service |

> **Practitioner Note:** Deliver makes value measurable. Without this stage, services can operate perfectly on technical metrics while failing to achieve their business purpose — a gap ITIL 4 did not formally close.

---

## Stage 8: Support

Sustain the service over its operational life. Continual Improvement moves from a parallel track to an embedded stage, triggering new lifecycle iterations.

**Key Activities:**
- Conduct Continual Improvement reviews and register improvements
- Manage the service throughout its agreed lifecycle until retirement
- Feed improvement opportunities back into Discover for the next iteration
- Maintain knowledge articles and service documentation currency
- Plan and execute service retirement or replacement when appropriate

### Learning Objectives — Stage 8: Support

- Describe the role of Continual Improvement within the Support stage
- Explain how Support connects to the Discover stage to restart the lifecycle
- Apply the Continual Improvement Model to a service scenario
- Identify when a service should move towards retirement

### Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Continual Improvement** | Ongoing activity to align and improve services with changing business needs |
| **Improvement Register** | Documented log of identified improvements and their status |
| **Service Retirement** | Planned withdrawal of a service from production |
| **Knowledge Management** | Practice ensuring the right knowledge is available to the right people at the right time |

> **Practitioner Note:** Support closes the loop. Every improvement identified here should re-enter the lifecycle at Discover with a validated business case — creating the continuous delivery loop ITIL (Version 5) is built on.

# Section 3: The 6C AI Capability Model

The 6C AI Capability Model is the most visible part of the 36% genuinely new content in ITIL (Version 5). It gives IT leaders a structured way to assess where AI is being used in their service operations, what capabilities they have built, and where the gaps are.

The six Cs are not a maturity model in sequence — organisations may be advanced in Communication (virtual agents) while still nascent in Cognition (predictive analytics). Use the model as an audit tool, a planning lens, and a governance framework.

---

### C1 — Creation

Generating new content, configurations, or solutions using AI. Includes AI-assisted drafting of knowledge articles, automated service designs, and generative troubleshooting guides.

**Practical examples:**
- AI generates first-draft knowledge articles from incident data
- Automated service design suggestions based on demand patterns
- Generative AI produces SLA draft language from stakeholder inputs

---

### C2 — Curation

Filtering, organising, and maintaining AI-generated or AI-assisted outputs to ensure quality, relevance, and governance compliance.

**Practical examples:**
- Human review of AI-generated knowledge articles before publication
- Automated deduplication and categorisation of the knowledge base
- Quality scoring of AI outputs against defined standards

---

### C3 — Clarification

Using AI to interpret ambiguous requests or surface intent from noisy data, improving the accuracy of service management responses.

**Practical examples:**
- Virtual agent disambiguates vague incident descriptions
- NLP classifies email requests to correct service queues
- AI suggests request categories when users are uncertain

---

### C4 — Cognition

AI-driven analysis, pattern recognition, and decision support. Supports problem management, trend analysis, and predictive operations.

**Practical examples:**
- Predictive alerting identifies failure patterns before incidents
- Problem Management AI identifies recurring root causes across incidents
- Capacity planning models predict demand spikes from usage trends

---

### C5 — Communication

AI-mediated interactions between users and service management systems, including virtual agents, chatbots, and automated status updates.

**Practical examples:**
- Service desk virtual agent handles Tier 1 requests autonomously
- Automated incident status notifications reduce inbound call volume
- AI translates technical jargon into user-friendly updates

---

### C6 — Coordination

Orchestrating AI agents and human actors across service workflows. Includes governance of AI decision-making and escalation when AI coordination fails.

**Practical examples:**
- AI orchestrator routes complex incidents across resolver groups
- Automated change approval for pre-approved standard changes
- AI coordinates cross-supplier incident bridges and follow-ups

---

## 3.1 AI Governance Obligations

The 6C model introduces formal AI governance obligations within ITIL (Version 5). Organisations deploying AI in service operations must now address:

- **Accountability for AI outputs** - who is responsible when an AI decision causes harm or service failure?
- **Transparency requirements** - users must be informed when they are interacting with AI systems
- **Escalation paths** - defined procedures when AI coordination fails or produces unexpected results
- **Audit trails** - AI decision logs must be retained for review and compliance purposes

> **Governance Note**
>
> If you are deploying AI in your service desk today without a governance framework, ITIL (Version 5) has just given you both the requirement and the vocabulary to build one. The 6C model is also a retrospective audit tool — map your existing AI tools against each C to identify governance gaps before they become incidents.

# Section 4: Management Practices

All 34 ITIL 4 management practices are retained in ITIL (Version 5). The Technical Management Practices group was removed as a category; those practices have been repositioned under the Product and Service Management group. No practices were eliminated.

## 4.1 Practice Groups

| Practice Group | Scope |
|---|---|
| General Management Practices (14) | Broad organisational management — strategy, workforce, risk, continual improvement |
| Product & Service Management Practices (20) | Service-specific delivery, including former Technical Management Practices |

## 4.2 Core Practices for Foundation Level

The following practices carry the highest weight in the ITIL (Version 5) Foundation exam and are most frequently applied in daily service management:

| Practice | Key Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Continual Improvement** | Ongoing alignment of services with business needs; now embedded in the Support stage |
| **Change Enablement** | Assess, authorise, and implement changes with minimal risk |
| **Incident Management** | Restore service to agreed levels as quickly as possible |
| **Problem Management** | Reduce likelihood and impact of incidents by eliminating root causes |
| **Service Request Management** | Handle routine requests efficiently without change control overhead |
| **Service Desk** | Single point of contact between users and the service provider |
| **Service Level Management** | Set, monitor, and report on service level agreements |
| **Monitoring & Event Management** | Detect and respond to events across the infrastructure |
| **Release Management** | Make new and changed services available for use |
| **Knowledge Management** | Ensure the right knowledge is available to the right people at the right time |
| **IT Asset Management** | Plan, manage, and dispose of IT assets throughout their lifecycle |
| **Service Configuration Management** | Maintain accurate information about all CIs and their relationships |

## 4.3 Learning Objectives for Practices

At Foundation level, candidates are expected to:

- Recall the purpose of each of the 12 core practices listed above
- Describe the key inputs and outputs of Incident, Problem, and Change Enablement practices
- Explain how the Service Desk practice differs from a help desk model
- Apply the Continual Improvement model to a described scenario
- Distinguish between the scope of Service Level Management and Service Request Management

# Section 5: Study Guide and Exam Preparation

## 5.1 Exam Format Summary

> **Foundation Exam Details**
>
> - **Format:** 40 multiple-choice questions (one correct answer per question)
> - **Duration:** 60 minutes (75 minutes for non-native speakers in some regions)
> - **Pass mark:** 65% — you need 26 correct answers out of 40
> - **Closed book:** No reference materials permitted
> - **Delivery:** Online proctored or at an accredited test centre
> - **Validity:** Certificate issued with a 3-year renewal date

## 5.2 Topic Weighting

Foundation exam questions are distributed across the syllabus areas. Based on the published syllabus structure, expect the following approximate distribution:

| Syllabus Area | Approximate Weighting |
|---|---|
| Key concepts and terminology | ~20% |
| The 8-Stage Lifecycle (all stages) | ~30% |
| The 7 Guiding Principles | ~15% |
| Management Practices (core 12) | ~20% |
| The 6C AI Capability Model | ~10% |
| SVS, Four Dimensions, governance | ~5% |

## 5.3 Recommended Study Path

This training document is designed to be worked through in stages. Below is a recommended 3-week self-study plan:

### Week 1 — Foundation Concepts

- Read Section 1 (Introduction) in full
- Study the 7 Guiding Principles - memorise names and know how to apply each
- Study the Four Dimensions Model - be able to categorise examples
- Learn the SVS structure and how it relates to the lifecycle
- Complete 20 practice questions on key concepts

### Week 2 — The 8-Stage Lifecycle

- Work through Section 2 stage by stage — one stage per day
- For each stage: learn the purpose, key inputs/outputs, and gate criteria
- Draw the lifecycle from memory - practice until confident
- Focus on distinguishing adjacent stages (e.g., Operate vs Deliver)
- Complete 30 practice questions focused on lifecycle scenarios

### Week 3 — Practices, AI Model & Exam Prep

- Study Section 3 (6C AI Model) - understand each C with examples
- Study Section 4 (Practices) - focus on the 12 core practices
- Complete two full mock exams under timed conditions
- Review all incorrect answers and revisit source sections
- Final read-through of key terms glossary (Section 6)

## 5.4 Exam Technique Tips

- Read every question twice - ITIL questions frequently hinge on a single word ('MOST likely', 'BEST describes', 'FIRST action')
- Eliminate obviously wrong answers - ITIL (Version 5) questions typically have one correct, one plausible, and two clearly incorrect options
- 'Value' is almost always the right lens - if two answers seem equally correct, choose the one that most directly relates to delivering value to stakeholders
- Watch for absolutes - answers with 'always', 'never', or 'only' are usually incorrect in ITIL contexts
- The 7 Guiding Principles are frequently tested as applied scenarios — know how each applies in practice, not just the name

## 5.5 Common Mistakes to Avoid

> **Watch Out For These**
>
> - Confusing Incident with Problem - Incident = restore service now; Problem = find and fix root cause
> - Applying rigid sequencing to the lifecycle - stages can iterate; they are not always strictly linear
> - Treating the 6C model as a maturity scale - it is an audit/planning tool, not a sequential path
> - Forgetting that ITIL 4 Foundation is still a valid prerequisite - no need to retake it
> - Assuming 'Operate' and 'Deliver' are the same - Operate is technical performance; Deliver is value realisation

# Section 6: Key Terms Glossary

Foundation candidates should be able to provide a working definition of each term and distinguish similar terms (e.g., Incident vs Problem, Output vs Outcome).

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **AI Agent** | An automated software entity that performs service management tasks with varying degrees of autonomy |
| **Change Enablement** | Practice ensuring changes are assessed, authorised, prioritised, planned, tested, and implemented with minimum risk |
| **Configuration Item (CI)** | Any component that needs to be managed in order to deliver a service |
| **Continual Improvement** | An ongoing organisational activity to align services and practices with changing business needs |
| **Demand Signal** | An indication of a business need or opportunity that initiates a new lifecycle iteration |
| **Digital Product** | A product delivered primarily through digital channels and maintained as a living service |
| **Event Management** | Practice monitoring all services and CIs, and filtering events requiring action |
| **Four Dimensions Model** | The four perspectives essential to balanced service management: People & Orgs, Info & Tech, Partners & Suppliers, Value Streams & Processes |
| **Incident** | An unplanned interruption to a service, or reduction in the quality of a service |
| **Known Error** | A problem that has been analysed and has not been resolved but has a documented workaround |
| **Opportunity Register** | A documented log of identified potential new services or improvement opportunities |
| **Outcome** | A result for a stakeholder enabled by one or more outputs — the measure of value realisation |
| **Output** | A tangible or intangible deliverable produced by an activity |
| **PeopleCert** | The sole governing body for ITIL, PRINCE2, and related frameworks following absorption of Axelos |
| **Problem** | A cause — or potential cause — of one or more incidents |
| **Product and Service Lifecycle** | The 8-stage model (Discover → Design → Acquire → Build → Transition → Operate → Deliver → Support) at the core of ITIL (Version 5) |
| **Release Management** | Practice planning, scheduling, and controlling the movement of releases to live environments |
| **Service** | A means of enabling value co-creation by facilitating outcomes customers want to achieve |
| **Service Level Agreement (SLA)** | A documented agreement between provider and customer defining service levels |
| **Service Value System (SVS)** | The overarching ITIL model showing how components and activities combine to create value |

| **Underpinning Agreement (UA)** | A contract or agreement with a third party that supports an SLA commitment |
| **Utility** | Functionality offered by a service — 'fit for purpose' |
| **Value Co-creation** | The collaborative process through which provider and customer together create value |
| **Value Realisation** | The extent to which intended business outcomes have been achieved |
| **Warranty** | Assurance that a service will meet agreed requirements — 'fit for use' |
